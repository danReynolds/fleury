## Why Fleury

<CardGrid>
  <FeatureCard icon="puzzle" title="A Real Widget Tree, for Cells" href="/fleury/architecture/core-and-targets/" cta="How it fits together">
    A widget, element, and render tree with a real layout engine — painting to a
    grid of cells, not pixels. Rebuilds stay on the dirty subtree, so an idle app
    emits nothing.
  </FeatureCard>
  <FeatureCard icon="open-book" title="Built for Agents" href="/fleury/guides/driving-with-agents/" cta="Drive it with an agent">
    Every widget publishes its role, state, and actions to a live semantic tree.
    Agents drive your app through it over MCP — and your tests assert on the same
    tree — instead of scraping the screen. Nothing extra to wire up.
  </FeatureCard>
  <FeatureCard icon="laptop" title="One App, Multiple Targets" href="/fleury/architecture/serving-and-embedding/" cta="Serving & embedding">
    The same widget tree renders natively to a terminal, or to the browser —
    compiled in with dart2js, or streamed live by <code>fleury serve</code>. A
    parity oracle diffs the targets so they can't drift.
  </FeatureCard>
  <FeatureCard icon="rocket" title="Fast Development & Debugging" href="/fleury/guides/hot-reload/" cta="Hot reload">
    Stateful hot reload on save — rare in a terminal — so a running app picks up
    your edits in place, state intact. And a debug shell in every dev run:
    Ctrl+G for frame timings, paint-flash, the live semantic tree, logs and
    errors — right there, even over SSH.
  </FeatureCard>
</CardGrid>

## How it works

*One widget tree, two surfaces* rests on a retained-mode engine: it turns your
widgets into cells and paints them wherever you point it — a terminal, a
browser, a streamed session. These two pages walk the whole pipeline.
